Historic Knot Facts

The humble knot that we have known ever sense we could tie our shoes has a surprisingly long history, dating back at least 15,000 years. Initial indications point to the use of rudimentary knots for securing tools and constructing shelters. As civilizations advanced, more sophisticated knotting techniques emerged, aiding in endeavors like fishing, navigation, and information recording. Despite ongoing debates about the precise knot types and their cultural meanings, the prehistoric narrative of knots unveils a captivating tale of human creativity and technological evolution spanning millennia.

Fun facts

Some scientists have speculated that the first knotters were animals, gorillas who tied simple “granny knots,” interlacing branches to construct nests. - New York Times, In Praise of the Humble Knot
The hagfish is known to strip slime from its skin by tying itself into a simple overhand knot, and moving its body to make the knot travel toward the tail. It also uses this action in reverse (tail to head) to pry out flesh after biting into a carcass.
Knot-tying has been around since early human history. We began creating tools with rope. The creation of knots predates other simple tools like axes and the wheel. Some historians think knot-tying may even predate the discovery of fire.
The oldest fossil records of ropes are from 13,000 BC, about 15,000-17,000 years ago. Historians believe ancient peoples used knots long before that, but that we don’t have a record of it.

Summary of Knot Theory

Synopsis of Algorithm: Knot theory is the branch of mathematics that studies geometric knots. Alan Turing thought the “Knot Equivalence Problem” probably undecidable”. Wolfgang Haken created a computer algorithm in 1961 that can determine if any closed shape is a knot or not, but for large shapes can take up to the heat death of the universe to calculate. Other identifiers of knots include p-colorability and Dowker-Thistlethwaite algorithmic notation, and while the identifiers are not mutually exclusive, for 10 crossing knots and lower they can be used for the knot equivalence problem.
